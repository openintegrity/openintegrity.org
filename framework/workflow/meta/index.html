Harnessing Crowds: Mapping the Genome of Collective Intelligence

This paper proposes an interesting reading grid for how Open Integrity positions itself in terms of a participative effort.

Who?

In the question of "who?" the paper proposes Hierarchy and Crowd as possible "Genes". In that sense Open Integrity is both an expertise led effort - where community of practices are an organising principle - and a Crowd-led or open participation project in the sense that it doesn't enforce any control on who can participate and allows anonymous participation.

As a consequence there should be clear rules of engagement (including a meritocratic assignment of moderation roles) and an enforced code of conduct.

Why?

The question of incentives is one that we've been thinking about a lot. The question is posed for each of the potential participants in the OII effort which are:

  • Advisory: who help steer and evaluate the overall success of the initiative
  • Practice Experts: who help establish what best practices are and how to measure their adoption
  • Contributors and Reviewers: who submit data or claims and help review them

In addition more broadly, in the Open Integrity Ecosystem we engage with the following stakeholders in interactions that also have participative and collective intelligence aspects:

  • Tool Developers: by recommending best practices, helping self-assess, accessing support to adopt them.
  • Advocates and Educators: by making consistent data available about complex technical issues and helping them to use this data for advocacy or education with a range of audiences (journalists, human rights defenders, activists,...), through a range of channels (web platforms, face to face education efforts, mobile apps, app stores,...).
  • Other "System-level" stakeholders such as funders, investors, policy makers: by providing metrics for various aspects of software ecosystems and stakeholders.

We'll develop our thoughts progressively on incentives on this platform, and use the paper's "Genes" - Money, Love, Glory - as a grid to exercise our thinking.

What? How?

Again different part of Open Integrity have different approaches with regards to what the collective effort aims for. Broadly speaking:

  • the Data collaboration is more about creation with:
    • Collection being a focus for the main part
    • Collaboration playing out when the peer review process kicks in.
  • the Framework collaboration is about decision
    • Consensus being the main way in which best practices debates operates
    • Individual decisions being at play with regards to the development of measurement approaches and metrics with a "market" dynamic but not monetary, rather based on data demand and feedback on metrics quality or performance (how well it matches multiple independent expert assesments for instance).
